1. General Project Mission Statement & Ethos
Rooted in the Extended Mind Hypothesis (Clark & Chalmers)—the idea that tools aren't just aids to cognitive processes, but literal extensions of the human mind. The Friction keyboard is not just an input device; it is an active, empathetic participant in managing the user's cognitive load. It introduces physical resistance to digital environments, ensuring that technology adapts to the limits of human biology, rather than forcing human biology to break for the sake of digital productivity.
Previously, technological "extensions" of the mind have been ruthlessly optimized for speed and output, lacking a tangible form that actually adapts to the human using them. Friction pivots this relationship entirely. Instead of extracting maximum productivity, we are focused on how your brain reacts to work at a fundamental, chemical, and brainwave level. The keyboard physically morphs in real-time to mirror your cognitive state, building profound self-awareness of how your brain is feeling in any given situation. Whether it's helping you seamlessly pick up exactly where you left off after an interruption or gently guiding you to a physical stop before the onset of burnout, Friction ensures your tools care for your mind as much as your work.
2. Main Mission of the Product
To act as a physical and digital "focus prosthetic" that intercepts burnout before the user consciously feels it. Traditional "quantified self" wearables often create a self-fueling cycle of panic: you become anxious simply because your watch notified you that your anxiety is up 3% today. Friction deliberately dismantles this cycle.
By using targeted, nuanced physical friction and guided digital reflection, Friction shifts the paradigm from stressful, hyper-quantified dashboards to "qualitative scaffolding" (subconscious physical nudges). The reflection components are highly intentional and are explicitly designed to prevent constant checking. Data is never represented as a raw number or an arbitrary score. Instead, reflections are encouraged as structured end-of-day or weekly rituals. The system provides various guided reflection paths to help the user unpack their mental states and workflows (with an expanding library of paths planned post-MVP), ensuring the focus remains on holistic self-awareness rather than metric-induced anxiety.
3. User Flow & Archetypes (The Session Journey)
The true innovation of the Friction keyboard lies in how the system translates the raw data of human cognition into empathetic, physical interventions. To understand this, we examine three distinct states of high-cognitive-load work, mapping directly to our three digital touchpoints:
Claudia (The Freelancer) & "The Taper" (Influenced by Page 1: The Terminal)
The Challenge: Context-switching and the jarring "wired-to-unplugged" dissociation of creative burnout.
The Setup: Claudia is deep into a four-hour session, splicing together a documentary timeline (focus_percent: 0.92).
The Intervention: At hour four, approaching the fluid time boundary she subconsciously set in the Terminal (Page 1) earlier that day, her fatigue_percent creeps to 0.75. Instead of a harsh alarm, Friction initiates The Taper. The keys beneath Claudia’s fingers gently lose their crisp snap, becoming slightly softer and "spongier." The RGB underglow shifts to a warm amber. Subconsciously, the physical resistance forces her frantic typing to slow down. Simultaneously, the app surfaces historical "low-fatigue" tasks (e.g., Organize B-roll folders). She is gently pulled out of flow, creating a soft landing for her brain.
The Science (Cognitive Exhaust & Glutamate Buildup): A groundbreaking 2022 study from the Paris Brain Institute demonstrated that intense, prolonged cognitive work causes a literal buildup of glutamate—a potentially toxic byproduct when accumulated—in the lateral prefrontal cortex. This neuro-metabolic accumulation makes further cognitive work incredibly difficult, requiring a period of "drainage" or rest to clear the synapses. Abruptly stopping high-cognitive-load work shocks the nervous system, exacerbating burnout. The Taper acts as an active cognitive cool-down, gently easing the brain into its necessary refractory period so it can clear this buildup and recover efficiently. A neuro-metabolic account of why daylong cognitive work alters the control of economic decisions (Wiehler et al., 2022)
Perplexous (The PhD Student) & "Flow State Preservation" (Assisted by Page 2: The Scaffolding)
The Challenge: Holding massive, fragile mental models, and the devastating cost of interruptions.
The Setup: Perplexous is debugging a multi-omics data pipeline (focus_percent: 0.98). A lab mate bursts in, shattering his attention (focus_percent drops to 0.05).
The Intervention: Normally, the "Where was I?" tax would cost him twenty minutes. But as he turns back to his screen, the Scaffolding (Page 2) visualizes his attention residue as an organic "digital moss" creeping across his workspace. Etched into this moss is an AI-synthesized summary of his last keystrokes: "You were adjusting the normalization weights. The last variable modified was batch_variance_threshold." He swipes his mouse to clear the moss, the words snap back into his code editor, and the cognitive bridge is instantly rebuilt.
The Science (The 23-Minute Rule & Attention Residue): A landmark study from UC Irvine (Mark et al., 2008) quantified the exact "context-switching tax," proving it takes an average of 23 minutes and 15 seconds to fully return to a primary task after being interrupted. This delay is driven by a phenomenon coined "attention residue" by Dr. Sophie Leroy (2009): when forced to switch tasks before completing the first, the brain keeps a portion of its processing power anchored to the unfinished task. By instantly externalizing his working memory through the AI summary, Friction bypasses the hippocampus's need to slowly reload the mental state, mitigating attention residue and effectively eliminating the context-switching tax. The cost of interrupted work (Mark et al., 2008)
Charlie (The Solo Lawyer) & "The Hard Intercept" (Reflecting in Page 3: The Mirror)
The Challenge: The late-night grind, ignoring bodily limits, and catastrophic fatigue.
The Setup: It is 2:15 AM. Charlie is cross-referencing antitrust discovery documents.
The Intervention: Charlie's fatigue_percent hits a critical 0.95. Friction stops asking nicely. The magnetic switches engage maximum resistance, making the keys incredibly heavy. On screen, the Progressive Vignette activates—a dark shadow creeps in from the edges of his monitor, literally shrinking his digital field of view. A notification locks the screen: "Cognitive capacity depleted. Physical reset required." The sheer effort required to type breaks the spell, saving him from a 4:00 AM crash.
The Morning After (Growing Self-Awareness): Crucially, the system does not force Charlie to reflect at 2:15 AM when his cognitive capacity is biologically compromised. Instead, when he logs on the next morning, Friction passively opens to The Mirror (Page 3). It gently notifies him of the biological realities behind last night's hard cutoff—displaying the heavy Gaussian blur on his timeline where his prefrontal cortex depleted. It doesn't present this as a scolding justification, but as a qualitative insight designed to organically grow his self-awareness around late-night fatigue so he can adjust his future workflow.
The Science (The Loss of Self-Awareness in Fatigue): A famous 2003 sleep study by Dinges and Van Dongen proved that as sleep deprivation compounds over hours and days, objective cognitive performance (reaction time, logic, error rates) drops linearly, but subjective feelings of sleepiness plateau. Charlie literally loses the neurological ability to realize how tired he actually is or how many errors he is making. The Hard Intercept uses physical resistance to bypass this biologically proven loss of self-awareness, overriding his degraded digital judgment to force a necessary physical reset. The cumulative cost of additional wakefulness (Van Dongen et al., 2003)
4. UI/UX Aesthetic Directions & Interactions
We are developing two distinct visual directions to encompass the product's identity:
Aesthetic Direction A: "The Invisible Clinic" (Apple-esque / Ethereal)
Vibe: Sleek, frictionless, calming, sterile. The UI feels like a high-end medical device designed by a luxury brand.
Visuals: Frost white, brushed silver, translucent glassmorphism (heavy background blur), glowing cyan/blue indicators. Smooth, breathing animations.
Aesthetic Direction B: "The Brutalist Tactile" (Dune / Retro-Futurism)
Vibe: Heavy, grounded, mechanical, slightly intimidating. Focuses on the "weight" of the data.
Visuals: Matte black, dark charcoal, amber/orange glowing text, grainy/noisy textures. UI elements look like physical switches with snappy, high-contrast states.
5. App Page Architecture (The Micro-Interactions)
Friction's interface is designed to feel like an organic extension of the mind, completely devoid of traditional software clunkiness.
Page 1: The Terminal (Setup & Intent)
Mission: Cognitive Offloading. To absorb the user's scattered intent and translate it into a biologically sustainable timeline, eliminating the anxiety of self-calculating cognitive load.
Task Ingestion (Multi-Modal): Users can populate their canvas via manual typing, automatic syncs with Notion/Jira/Figma, or a simple "Voice/Text Brain Dump" where the local AI parses a stream-of-consciousness into distinct task nodes.
Fluid Time Boundary (Frictionless Scheduling): Setting a time limit for the session is entirely ambient. Instead of interacting with rigid drop-down menus or typing "120 minutes," users can simply state "I have two hours" during a voice dump, or physically stretch the borders of their timeline canvas to expand the session. The system also introduces biological guardrails: if a user tries to stretch a session to 5 hours without a break, the timeline border physically resists the cursor, gently signaling that the requested temporal boundary exceeds their historical cognitive stamina.
The SNN "Weighting" System: Users do not assign difficulty to tasks. The localized SNN analyzes historical biometric data to automatically assign a "Cognitive Weight" to each node. High-load tasks (e.g., coding) are visualized through a deeper monochromatic gradient. When dragging a heavy node, the UI applies a subtle inertia—it doesn't feel sluggish, but rather "anchored," giving the user a subconscious tactile feel for the cognitive demand of the task without inducing anxiety.
Timeline Views (Customizable Framing):
Level 1 (Super Obvious): A standard linear, chronologic calendar timeline.
Level 2 (The Battery Stack): A vertical stack. As users drop heavy tasks into the stack, it fills up a meter based on their biological limit for the day, visually preventing over-scheduling.
Level 3 (Super Abstract): "The Energy Pool." A fluid, amorphous simulation where task nodes float. As total cognitive load increases, the fluid viscosity visually thickens, representing the mental density of the day.
Page 2: The Scaffolding (Live Session)
Mission: Symbiotic Preservation. To act as a dynamic, tactile safety net that responds to the exact neurological needs of the user across six distinct sub-states of active work.
Sub-State 1: Absolute Invisibility (Max Focus) When focus_percent is peaking (>0.90), the digital UI ceases to exist. There are no breathing dots, no edge sliders, and no docks. Absolute digital silence. The screen belongs entirely to the user's active application. The only indicator that the system is active is physical: the keyboard chassis emits a sub-perceptual binaural hum synced to the user's alpha brainwaves to sustain the flow state.
Sub-State 2: The Tactile Strike (Task Completion in Max Focus) When a user finishes a task while in peak flow, summoning a digital checklist would fracture their attention. Instead, task completion is handled physically. The user executes a "Tactile Strike"—a heavy press on a dedicated, highly-resistant macro key on the Friction keyboard. This triggers a satisfying localized haptic thud. On-screen, a transient glassmorphic ripple temporarily materializes over the active window, displays a minimalist checkmark and the title of the next queued task for exactly 1.5 seconds, and then dissolves back into pure invisibility. Zero mouse movement required.
Sub-State 3: The Passive Task Bar (Moderate State / The Taper) When focus_percent begins to drift (0.50–0.70) or fatigue_percent rises, the system recognizes the user can no longer sustain heavy cognitive lifting. The UI gently surfaces as a minimal, translucent edge-slider or bottom dock. It quietly floats the "low-cognitive weight" tasks (e.g., answering emails, organizing files) that the SNN identified during Setup. Simultaneously, the keyboard's magnetic switches become slightly "spongier," subconsciously matching the user's moderate, tapering pace.
Sub-State 4: Active Recovery (Low Focus / The Trough) The user has lost focus entirely, but they haven't left the desk. Instead of letting them fall into the trap of doomscrolling, Friction initiates active biological recovery. The screen gently dims the active work window, surfacing physiological resets. The UI may prompt a breathing exercise—where an expanding/contracting visualizer syncs perfectly with the keyboard's ambient RGB pulsing. Alternatively, it prompts a 60-second "Power Pose" or desk stretch. Using the keyboard's embedded RF Volumetric mesh (which tracks spatial posture without cameras), the system actually verifies the user is performing the physical stretch before unlocking the high-focus UI again.
Sub-State 5: The Return (Post-Distraction & Flow Preservation) The user physically walked away or became completely distracted, and now they are back. Instead of a sterile "Resume" button, this state is visualized through the lens of nature reclaiming brutalist architecture. As the local SNN synthesizes a summary of where the user left off, an organic, fractal-like "digital moss" creeps inward from the bezels of the screen, wrapping around code editors and timelines like ivy. Etched into the negative space of this moss is a scattered word cloud of the exact high-impact keywords and phrases the user was just typing.
The Recovery Interaction: Returning to work feels akin to walking into an ancient rainforest and swiping away hanging cobwebs. The user sweeps their mouse across the screen to brush away the moss.
The Science Behind It:
Embodied Cognition: Swiping or rapidly shaking the mouse to clear the screen forces a deliberate kinetic action, which signals a "state change" to the brain faster than visual processing alone (Kirsh, 2013).
Spatial Memory: As the moss is cleared, the scattered words visually snap back into their exact original positions in the document. This utilizes the brain's spatial map as a cognitive shortcut to bypass "resumption lag" (Ratwani et al., 2008).
Multisensory Integration: The visual snap is accompanied by a subtle, satisfying haptic click from the keyboard, reducing the cognitive effort required to process the task recovery (Hayward et al., 2023).
Sub-State 6: The Hard Intercept (System Override & Slow Shutdown) When fatigue_percent hits critical mass (>0.95), the system stops suggesting and starts enforcing. Instead of a sudden, panic-inducing blackout, it initiates a 60-second "Slow Shutdown."
Physical Resistance: The keyboard actively fights the user. The magnetic switches engage maximum actuation force, making typing feel impossibly heavy—like pressing keys through molasses.
Visual Diminishment: The Progressive Vignette activates, casting a dark, creeping shadow from the bezels that literally shrinks the digital field of view to a claustrophobic pinpoint.
The Offload: During this 60-second grace period, the local SNN automatically saves the workspace state and prepares the "digital moss" packet for tomorrow morning. A final, high-contrast modal locks the center screen: "Cognitive capacity depleted. Workspace secured. Physical reset required." The system forces the user to disconnect before they make catastrophic fatigue-induced errors.
Page 3: The Mirror (Reflection)
Mission: Qualitative Synthesis. To dismantle metric-induced anxiety by visualizing the day not as a score to be beaten, but as a topography of mental energy to be understood.
UI Execution: A "Depth of Field" timeline. Instead of anxiety-inducing line graphs, the session is visualized through focus. Periods of peak flow (focus_percent > 0.8) are rendered in razor-sharp, crisp high-definition. Periods of burnout and high fatigue are represented by heavy Gaussian blur. Users map their tasks against this visual timeline, learning exactly which type of work drains them and what induces flow.

7. Tech Specs (The Near-Future Paradigm)
Input (The Brain Chip): The user has a standard-issue neural implant capable of reading complex biometric and brainwave data.
Processing (The Keyboard): The Friction keyboard contains a localized Spiking Neural Network (SNN) neuromorphic chip. It connects directly to the user's neural link.
The Data Bottleneck: To ensure absolute privacy, raw neural data never leaves the keyboard. The SNN processes the complex brainwaves locally and outputs exactly two simple data streams to the host computer/app:
focus_percent (Float: 0.0 - 1.0)
fatigue_percent (Float: 0.0 - 1.0)
engagement_precent (Float: 0.0 - 1.0)
Output (Nuanced Haptic Actuators):
Spongy Key Resistance: As fatigue sets in, magnetic key switches don't just get firmer—they get "spongy" and rise/breathe slower, as if the keyboard itself is getting tired, subtly forcing the user to type slower.
Ambient RGB: Soft, glowing light that shifts dynamically to reflect mental states.
Acoustic: Sub-perceptual binaural beats/humming built into the chassis to gently guide brainwaves.
8. Privacy & Safeguards
Architectural Privacy: By design, the host device and cloud only see three anonymous float values. The raw thoughts, private ideations, and neural patterns are trapped and destroyed inside the keyboard's isolated SNN.
Physical Kill Switch: A heavy, satisfying mechanical toggle on the side of the keyboard instantly severs the neural connection, reverting the device to a standard "dumb" analog input.
Data Burn Protocol: A mandatory end-of-week "Data Burn"—a three-second haptic hold that physically dissolves all cached session metrics to ensure zero long-term surveillance.
